The image displays a series of scatter plots that examine the relationship between track popularity and various audio features, including danceability, energy, speechiness, acousticness, instrumentalness, liveness, valence, and tempo. In each plot, the x-axis represents one of these features, while the y-axis tracks the popularity of each song, with values ranging from 0 to 100. These plots help identify any potential correlations or trends between the features and a track's popularity. Upon reviewing the plots, we can see some interesting trends. The danceability vs. popularity plot suggests a slight positive correlation, with more danceable tracks tending to be somewhat more popular. However, the relationship appears weak, as the data points are scattered. Similarly, energy vs. popularity shows a moderate positive trend, where tracks with higher energy levels seem to have a slightly better chance at achieving higher popularity, although the correlation is not very strong. In the case of speechiness vs. popularity, the plot reveals a mild positive relationship, indicating that tracks with more speech elements might be more popular, but the trend is not highly pronounced. The acousticness vs. popularity plot shows a weak negative correlation, with more acoustic tracks generally exhibiting lower popularity scores, suggesting that non-acoustic tracks are more likely to perform better. The scatter plot for instrumentalness vs. popularity also demonstrates a weak negative correlation, with instrumental tracks usually having lower popularity. This suggests that vocal tracks are more popular than instrumental ones. Similarly, the liveness vs. popularity plot points to a slightly negative relationship, with tracks that have a live sound tending to be less popular. The valence vs. popularity plot suggests a weak negative correlation, where more positive or happier tracks tend to have lower popularity, indicating that melancholic or darker tracks might be favored. Lastly, the tempo vs. popularity plot shows a very weak negative trend, suggesting that faster-paced songs do not necessarily perform better in terms of popularity.

The box plot visualizes the distribution of track popularity across different genre groups, with each box representing the most prominent genres. The height of the box shows the interquartile range (IQR) of popularity scores, while the horizontal line inside the box indicates the median popularity. The "whiskers" extending from the box represent the range of values, and any points outside the whiskers are considered outliers. The plot effectively highlights the spread and tendencies of track popularity for each genre group. In terms of specific genres, Motown shows a wide distribution with its median track popularity around 40, indicating a mixture of low and high popularity tracks within this genre. This makes sense given that the data includes tracks from years long ago where Motown was very popular whereas now not so much. Rock-and-roll shares a similar distribution with a median around 40, though its box is slightly smaller than that of Motown, suggesting less variation in popularity. This genre has stood the test of time moreso than Motown. British Invasion and Pop genres have higher median popularity, around 50 and 60 respectively, indicating more popular tracks within these genres. Country and Rap genres have a more concentrated distribution, with their popularity centering around the lower 50s to 60s, though Rap shows a few outliers with higher popularity scores. This comparison reveals how popularity varies across genre groups, with some genres tending to have more popular tracks on average than others.

This plot shows the density of songs across different genre groups over time, spanning from the 1950s to the 2020s. The x-axis represents the years, while the y-axis shows the number of songs released per genre group in each year. Different genre groups are represented by distinct colored lines: motown (blue), rock-and-roll (orange), pop (green), british invasion (purple), country (brown), and rap (red). From the graph, we can observe several key trends. Rock-and-roll and pop genres dominate in the mid-20th century, with a significant surge in popularity for rock-and-roll in the late 1950s and early 1960s, peaking in the 1970s. Pop music also shows a noticeable increase in the 1980s, continuing to rise steadily into the 2000s and 2010s. On the other hand, motown music shows more moderate growth, with its peak occurring in the 1960s, before declining sharply in the 1970s and remaining relatively low afterward. The british invasion genre sees its highest points in the 1960s, aligning with the era of British bands' like The Beatles, while country music maintains a steady, low but consistent presence throughout the decades, with a slight uptick in the 2000s. Rap music, however, shows a dramatic rise starting in the 1980s and exploding in popularity in the 2000s and 2010s, surpassing many of the other genre groups. Note here that rap music's trajectory contrasts with that of the other genres, indicating its substantial and relatively recent impact on the music industry. Overall, the graph illustrates landscape of music genres, with rock-and-roll and pop dominating the earlier decades, followed by the rise of rap in more recent years. This shift highlights the changing tastes and cultural influences over the decades, with certain genres like rock-and-roll and motown reaching their peak during specific cultural moments, while others like pop and rap have sustained long-term growth and remained '#1'.

Correlation Matrix Heatmap

Interactive Correlation Matrix of Audio Features

This heatmap displays the correlation between various Spotify audio features and track popularity (labeled as "track_pop"). The correlations range from -1 to 1, where values closer to 1 indicate a strong positive relationship, values closer to -1 indicate a strong negative relationship, and values near 0 suggest little to no correlation. The color gradient visualizes these correlations, with red representing positive correlations, blue representing negative correlations, and lighter shades indicating weaker relationships. From the heatmap, valence (a measure of musical positivity) exhibits the strongest positive correlation with track popularity (0.41), suggesting that songs with happier or more uplifting tones tend to be more popular. Danceability (0.16), energy (0.15), and speechiness (0.15) also show mild positive correlations, indicating that tracks with these characteristics slightly tend to resonate better with listeners. On the other hand, acousticness (-0.26) has the most notable negative correlation with popularity, implying that highly acoustic songs are less likely to achieve widespread popularity. Similarly, instrumentalness (-0.10) shows a weak negative correlation, meaning tracks that are purely instrumental are slightly less likely to be popular. Otsher features, such as liveness (-0.05) and tempo (-0.01), show negligible or no meaningful relationship with track popularity. In summary, the heatmap highlights that energetic, danceable, and emotionally positive songs are generally more popular, while highly acoustic or instrumental tracks are less so. However, the correlations are relatively modest, suggesting that while audio features influence popularity, other factors like artist recognition, marketing, and cultural trends also play significant roles.

Some of the key takeaways from our data analysis are that there are a sort of mixed bag of genres or mostly blends of genres that make up the Billboard Top 100 year over year. Additionally some of the key ingredients in terms of audio quality that make up a hit song are danceability, valence, and energy.
